# The r-process in equations:

**Neutron capture:** $$^A_Z X + n \rightarrow ^{A+1}_Z X + \gamma$$

Nucleus X with mass number A captures a neutron, becomes heavier isotope. Gamma ray released.

**Beta decay:** $$^{A+1}_Z X \rightarrow ^{A+1}_{Z+1} Y + e^- + \bar{\nu}_e$$

Unstable nucleus decays — neutron converts to proton, atomic number Z increases by 1, new element Y created. Electron and antineutrino emitted.

**The r-process condition:**

$$\tau_{capture} \ll \tau_{decay}$$

==Capture timescale much smaller than decay timescale==. Neutrons arrive faster than the nucleus can decay. This is what makes it rapid.

The two timescales:

**Neutron capture — r-process:**

$τ_{capture}∼10^3 - 10^2 s$

**Beta decay:**

$τ_{decay}$  ∼seconds to minutes for unstable isotopesτdecay​∼seconds to minutes for unstable isotopes

So the ratio:

$\frac{τ_{decay}}{τ_{capture}}∼ 10^2 - 10^5$

**In a neutron star merger the neutron density is:**

$$n_n \sim 10^{24} \text{ cm}^{-3}$$

That's $10^{24}$ free neutrons per cubic centimetre. For comparison, ordinary stellar interiors have maybe $10^{10}$. That's 14 orders of magnitude more neutrons — which is why only mergers and certain supernovae can drive the r-process.

**The endpoint** — you build up nuclei until you hit the neutron drip line, where nuclei can't hold any more neutrons, then decay cascades back down to stable heavy elements like Ba, Eu, Au, Pt, U.

Our star has the fingerprint of that entire chain locked in its spectrum.

# Element formation

So the nucleus doesn't get a chance to decay between captures.

It just keeps absorbing neutrons — mass number climbing rapidly — building heavier and heavier isotopes faster than nature can stabilise them.

Like this:

$$^{56}_{26}Fe \xrightarrow{+n} ^{57}_{26}Fe \xrightarrow{+n} ^{58}_{26}Fe \xrightarrow{+n} ^{59}_{26}Fe \xrightarrow{+n} \cdots$$

Iron just keeps getting heavier. In normal conditions it would decay at some point and become a new element. But the neutrons are arriving so fast it doesn't get the chance — it just keeps climbing the mass ladder.

Eventually it gets so neutron-rich it hits the drip line — the point where the nucleus physically cannot hold another neutron. Only then does it beta decay:

$$^{A}_{Z}X \rightarrow ^{A}_{Z+1}Y$$

Atomic number jumps up by 1 — new element born. Then another neutron capture begins on the new element. And so on.

The whole chain from iron to barium to europium to gold to uranium happens in **milliseconds** during the merger. Then the ejecta flies out into space, decays to stable isotopes over days to weeks, and eventually gets incorporated into gas clouds that form new stars.

Our star is the end product of that millisecond process.
