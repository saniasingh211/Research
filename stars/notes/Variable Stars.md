# An introduction

**A variable star is a star whose brightness changes over time.**

**Reason 1: Something is blocking the light.** A companion star, or a planet, passes in front. You see a dip in brightness periodically. This is an eclipsing binary — or in the exoplanet case, a transit. You already know this one.

**Reason 2: The star itself is physically changing.** The star expands and contracts — it pulses. As it gets bigger it gets dimmer, as it gets smaller it gets brighter. These are called pulsating variables.

---

The most famous pulsating variables are **Cepheids.**

And here's why they matter — this is one of the most beautiful results in all of astronomy:

**The period of a Cepheid's pulsation tells you its absolute brightness.**

Slow pulse = intrinsically bright star. Fast pulse = intrinsically dim star. Fixed relationship, like a law of nature.

So if you measure the period — which is easy, just watch it blink — you know how bright it truly is. Compare that to how bright it looks from Earth, and you get the distance.

**Cepheids are how we measure distances across the universe.** They're called standard candles.


> [!NOTE] History 
> Henrietta Swan Leavitt discovered it in 1908 by staring at photographic plates of the Small Magellanic Cloud — a nearby galaxy. She noticed that the brighter Cepheids pulsed more slowly. She had no telescope, no computer, just plates and patience. That relationship — period equals luminosity — is now called the Leavitt Law. It's how Hubble proved in 1924 that the Andromeda "nebula" was actually a separate galaxy millions of light years away. Before that, nobody knew the universe extended beyond the Milky Way. One woman, photographic plates, and a pattern nobody else noticed. That's it.
